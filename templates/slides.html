The Problem

  • Most offensive methodologies import assumptions from Unix, Windows, and cloud environments.
  • These assumptions fail on mainframe operating systems—silently and completely.
  • Assessments miss the real attack surface because testers look for things that don't exist here.

Five assumptions. Five failures. Let's walk through them.

VTAM
“Ports define exposure.”

VTAM's session fabric exists independently of TCP/IP. Logical units bind applications to terminals through a network layer that predates sockets. Sessions outlive connections. An nmap scan tells you almost nothing about what's reachable.

RACF
“There is a root user.”

RACF distributes authority across profiles, not accounts. There is no omnipotent superuser. Access is granted through resource profiles, group connections, and program control. Privilege is contextual, scoped, and auditable.

TSO
“Processes are short-lived.”

Address spaces persist for weeks or months. Identity is bound at startup and does not change. A started task runs under its assigned identity for the lifetime of the IPL. There is no fork, no exec, no PID recycling.

JES
“Work executes immediately.”

JES queues, schedules, and defers execution. A submitted job enters a spool, waits for an initiator, and runs when resources allow. The submitter's identity is preserved from submission through execution—even hours later.

TSO
“There is a filesystem.”

Datasets, catalogs, PDS members. No /etc, no hierarchy, no inodes. Data is organized by 44-character qualified names, managed through catalogs, and allocated in tracks and cylinders. The model is closer to a database than a filesystem.
